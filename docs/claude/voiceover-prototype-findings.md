# Voiceover Prototype Findings

**Date:** 2026-03-07
**Phase:** 1 — Diagnostic Prototype

## Overview

We built a diagnostic tool (`tools/voiceover_diagnostic.py`) that extracts
frames from a video at 2 fps, computes frame-to-frame pixel differences,
identifies transition candidates via spike detection, and optionally runs
OCR + fuzzy matching against parsed slide content.

Two test videos were analyzed:
- **Video #2** — Iteration Patterns Part 2 (short, 354s, 3 slides)
- **Video #4** — Comprehensions (longer, 1157s, 12 slides)

## Key Findings

### 1. Frame Differencing Produces a Clean Signal

The frame-difference plot shows a very clear pattern:
- Most frames have near-zero difference (static or minor typing changes)
- Slide transitions produce sharp, distinct spikes
- The spikes are 1-2 orders of magnitude above the noise floor

**However**, the absolute magnitude is low. For a mostly-white RISE slide
on a 3840x2160 display, even a full slide change produces a normalized
mean difference of only 0.01-0.05. The initial default threshold of 0.05
was too high and detected zero candidates. A threshold of 0.005 works well.

**Recommendation:** Auto-calibrate thresholds based on the video's actual
difference distribution (e.g., use a percentile-based approach rather than
a fixed absolute value).

### 2. Within-Slide Changes Can Be as Large as Transitions

In Video #4, some within-slide events (code execution output appearing)
produced spikes of 0.028 — comparable to actual slide transitions. This
means frame differencing alone cannot distinguish slide transitions from
large within-slide changes.

**Recommendation:** Frame differencing is a **pre-filter** to identify
candidate moments for OCR. The OCR + fuzzy matching step is essential for
confirming whether a candidate is a real slide transition.

### 3. OCR + Fuzzy Matching Is Highly Reliable

Tesseract OCR on the 3840x2160 RISE frames produces very readable text.
The `rapidfuzz` token-set-ratio scores are:
- **90-100** for correct slide matches
- **40-70** for incorrect matches
- Typical gap between best and runner-up: 20-40 points

Even with RISE chrome in the frame (X button, ?, slide numbers), the
matching works reliably.

### 4. RISE Slide Numbers Are Visible

RISE displays slide numbers in the bottom-right corner (format: `section.slide`,
e.g., `2.1`, `2.3`). This could be an additional matching signal — OCR just
that corner region. Not needed for the initial implementation but a useful
future enhancement.

### 5. Transition Animations Produce Multi-Frame Spikes

A single slide transition in RISE produces 2-3 consecutive high-difference
frames (spanning ~1-1.5 seconds). These need to be **clustered** into a
single transition event.

**Tested approach:** Merge candidates within a 3-second window. This
reduced 51 raw candidates to 31 clusters for Video #4.

### 6. Duplicate Slide Titles Require Sequential Constraint

Video #4 has slides with identical titles: [1] "Transformation" and [4]
"Transformation", [2] "Filterung" and [5] "Filterung". OCR alone cannot
distinguish them — the fuzzy match scores are nearly identical.

**The sequential ordering constraint is essential.** If the previous
confirmed slide was [3], the next "Transformation" must be [4], not [1].
This is the most important disambiguation mechanism.

### 7. Header/Title Slides Are Not in Parsed Slide Groups

The video starts with a header slide ("Komprehensionen: Elegantere
Iteration / Dr. Matthias Hölzl") generated by the `j2` macro. This slide
is not in the parsed slide list. The OCR matched it with a poor score
(54.0) against an unrelated slide.

**Recommendation:** The slide parser should recognize the header macro and
add a "header" slide group, or the matcher should detect low-confidence
OCR matches at the start of a video and treat them as title slides.

### 8. OCR 1 Second After Peak Works Well

Grabbing the frame 1 second after the peak difference (when the new slide
has stabilized after the transition animation) produces clean OCR results.

## Conclusions

**Strategy 4 (hybrid frame-diff + OCR) is validated.** The approach works
well for RISE screen recordings with live coding:

1. Frame differencing reliably identifies transition *candidates*
2. OCR + fuzzy matching reliably *confirms* which candidates are real
3. Sequential ordering resolves ambiguity from duplicate slide content
4. The pipeline is fast enough for practical use

## Implementation Implications

Based on these findings, the following adjustments to the design are needed:

1. **Auto-calibrate thresholds** — Use percentile-based detection (e.g.,
   flag frames above the 95th or 99th percentile of differences) rather
   than a fixed absolute threshold.

2. **Cluster nearby candidates** — Merge candidates within 2-3 seconds
   into single transition events, keeping the highest-confidence frame.

3. **OCR stabilized frame** — Grab the frame ~1 second after the detected
   spike peak, not the peak frame itself (which may be mid-transition).

4. **Sequential constraint is critical** — The matcher must enforce roughly
   monotonic slide ordering to handle duplicate slide titles.

5. **Handle header slides** — Either parse the `j2` header macro or detect
   and skip low-confidence initial frames.

6. **Threshold range** — Real slide differences on 3840x2160 RISE recordings
   are in the range 0.005-0.050, not 0.05-0.30 as initially assumed.
